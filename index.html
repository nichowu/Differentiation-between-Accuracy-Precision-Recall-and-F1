Differentiation between Accuracy, Precision, Recall and F1

 

Introduction

 

How many times have you missed an important email because the spam detector put it into “Spam” folder?  I’m not sure about you but for me, it happens at least once every couple of weeks. Have you ever wondered how accurate the spam detector really is? If I tell you that I have a spam detector that can correctly detect every single one of the spam emails, do you believe that you will never miss another important email?  This blog post will explain to you that it turns out, in the world of data science, accuracy isn’t as simple as you might have imagined. In fact, accuracy is only one of the four major metrics used in the world of data science. The other three are “Precision”, “Recall” and “F1”.

 

“Accuracy” is not a Good Metric

 

Before diving into “Precision”, “Recall” and “F1”, let’s first go over why “Accuracy” isn’t a good metric in most of the times. 

 

Suppose I have a lie detector that can help me identify whether a person is lying or not.  Out of 100 people, my lie detector successfully identified 99 non-liars but failed to pick out the only liar. So, the accuracy of my lie detector is (98+1)/100 = 99%. Will I be able to sell my lie detector to the police for a good price, based on this result? Probably not. What’s the problem here?

 

The problem is called class imbalance. In another word, there is too many non-liars and too few liars out of 100 people. Even though we got 99% accuracy, we failed to pick out the only important subject, the liar. 

 

This simple example aims to show you that “accuracy” isn’t a good metric, especially when it involves class imbalance in a situation. 

 

Now that we see the shortcomings of accuracy, what can we use as substitute? The answer depends on the situation.  

 

Recall

 

With the previous lie detector example, suppose that the most important thing for a lie detector is the ability to detect a liar. Anyone who isn’t a liar can be proven with other evidence later. Therefore, we don’t want to miss any liar, but we are okay to tolerate some degree of errors (identifying some non-liar as liar).  Out of the only liar, how many was it able to pick out? The answer is 0/1 = 0%. This rate is called recall rate. It focuses on the ability to pick out as many liars as possible (positive sample). 

 

Precision

 

Again, with the lie detector example, suppose the most important thing for a lie detector is not to incorrectly pick out some non-liar as liar, because anyone who gets classified as liar goes straight to the prison. Also suppose there were 95 non-liar and 5 liars, and the lie detector correctly identified 2 liars and incorrectly identified 3 non-liars. So, out of the 5 people that got picked out, how many of them are truly a liar? The answer is 2/(2+3) = 40%. This rate is called precision rate. It focuses on the ability to identify true liar. 

 

F1

 

When we want to focus on both the ability to pick out as many liars as possible and the ability to make identify true liars, we resort to F1 score. In another word, F1 score is just a combination of precision and recall scores. A good example is the judicial system where we want to catch every single criminal but at the same time, we wouldn’t want to incorrectly put an innocent people in jail. This is when we F1 score matters.

Applications in Real Life

 

Now that we have a basic understanding of the three “accuracy” matrices, let’s take a look at how they are actually put to use in real life. 

 

Disease Diagnose

 

Suppose a hospital just bought a state-of-the-art cancer detector. What would be an appropriate evaluation metric? We will consider patients detected with cancer as positive case.

 

Using “Precision” as an evaluation metric, our question becomes: 

Out of the patients that got identified with cancer, how many actually have cancers?

 

Using “Recall” as an evaluation metric, our question becomes: 

Out of the total number of cancer patients, how many actually got detected?

 

Using “Accuracy” as an evaluation metric, our question becomes: 

Out of the total number of patients that got tested, how many test results are correct?

 

Because the consequence of failing at detecting cancer for a patient is huge, we would rather be detecting healthy people with cancer (false positive), than failing to detect real cancer patients (false negative).  In other words, we want to keep false negatives as few as possible.  Therefore, “recall” should be considered as number one metric. 

 

 

Spam Detection

 

Going back to the beginning, suppose we are evaluating spam detection function for Gmail. What would be an appropriate evaluation metric? We will consider spam as positive case.

 

Using “Precision” as an evaluation metric, our question becomes: 

Out of the emails that got identified as spam, how many are actually spams?

 

Using “Recall” as an evaluation metric, our question becomes: 

Out of the total number of spams scanned, how many actually got detected?

 

Using “Accuracy” as an evaluation metric, our question becomes: 

Out of the total number of emails scanned, how many classifications are correct?

 

Would you rather seeing a spam in your inbox (false negative), or having a normal email get deleted by spam detector (false positive)? It is clear that the cost of false positive is much higher than false negative. Therefore, to keep false positive as few as possible, we should use “Precision” as the evaluation metric.
